# Nebraska Licensed Angler Survey — What Has Changed Over Time

**Executive summary for fisheries managers and administrators**
*Based on four statewide angler surveys: 2002, 2012, 2018, and 2025*

## Purpose and how to read this

Nebraska has surveyed licensed anglers four times over roughly two decades. This
report tracks the questions that were asked in a comparable way across those
surveys, so we can see how angler behavior, preferences, and attitudes have
shifted. The notes below translate the major trends into plain language and flag
what they may mean for management. Where survey questions or answer choices
changed between years (for example, the list of water-body types and information
sources), comparisons are less reliable and are not emphasized here.

A few percentage-point movements between any two surveys are normal noise; the
patterns highlighted below are the ones that hold up across multiple surveys or
represent a clear direction of travel.

## 1. More license holders are actually fishing

The share of license holders who reported **not** fishing in Nebraska fell from
about **17% (2002)** to **under 10% (2025)**. In other words, a larger fraction
of the people buying licenses are using them on the water. Among those who did
fish, the typical angler reported about **18–19 days on the water per year**, a
figure that has been remarkably stable across all four surveys (with a temporary
bump to ~23 days in 2018). The most avid anglers continue to fish 85–100+ days a
year.

**Why it matters:** participation per license is trending up, which is positive
for both license revenue and the relevance of the resource to the public.

## 2. How and where people fish is shifting

- **Bank and wading fishing remains the backbone** of Nebraska angling — roughly
  three-quarters to four-fifths of anglers fish this way, with little change.
- **Boat fishing is declining.** Use of a motorized boat fell steadily from
  about **67% (2002) to 52% (2025)**. At the same time, **kayaks and canoes have
  emerged** as a distinct and growing mode (about 15–17% in the two most recent
  surveys).
- **Distances traveled are modest and stable.** The typical angler's most-visited
  water is about 30 miles away one-way, and even favorite destinations are
  usually within ~50 miles.

**Why it matters:** access and amenities that support shore, wade, and small
non-motorized craft (bank access, parking, kayak launches) are serving a large
and, in the case of paddlecraft, growing portion of the angling public — not just
boat ramps.

## 3. What anglers want to catch is broadening

- **Walleye remains the single most preferred species**, but its share has slipped
  from about **31% (2002) to 24% (2025)**. Largemouth bass holds a steady second
  (~18–21%).
- **Channel catfish preference has declined** (from ~13% to ~7%), dropping from
  third to fifth.
- The biggest mover is the rise of anglers who say they **do not prefer any
  particular species** — up from about **10% to 18%**, now the third most common
  response. Crappie interest also edged up.

**Why it matters:** demand is becoming less concentrated on a few flagship
species. A meaningful and growing group of anglers are opportunity-driven
generalists who simply want to catch fish. Diverse, dependable fisheries — not
only premier walleye waters — support this audience.

## 4. Anglers are satisfied, and have been for years

Satisfaction improved notably between 2002 and 2012 and has held since. The share
who are **"very satisfied" has climbed every survey (16% → 28%)**, while the share
expressing any dissatisfaction has fallen from about a quarter of anglers to
roughly 15%. Overall satisfaction has been stable and positive across the last
three surveys.

**Why it matters:** the angling public's experience is good and steady — a solid
foundation to build on, and a useful baseline against which to watch for any
future erosion.

## 5. Why people fish: experience is gaining ground

When asked how important various reasons are for fishing, anglers consistently
rate **being outdoors, relaxation, and time with family and friends** as highly
important — and these **experience- and nature-based motivations have edged up**
over time. Reasons tied strictly to the **fishery itself (e.g., obtaining fish to
keep) have stayed flat and rank lower**.

**Why it matters:** for a large share of anglers, the quality of the overall
outdoor experience — clean, pleasant, accessible places to fish with others — is
as important as the catch itself. This supports investment in the broader setting,
not just fish stocking and harvest opportunity.

## 6. Attitudes: a gradual shift away from keeping fish

Anglers' orientation toward simply **catching something**, toward **catching
numbers** of fish, and toward **catching bigger fish** has been essentially stable
across the surveys (with interest in larger fish remaining the strongest of these
and ticking up slightly). The clearest movement is a **modest, steady decline in
how harvest-oriented anglers are** — fewer anglers are focused on keeping their
catch.

**Why it matters:** this is consistent with a long-running, gradual move toward a
catch-and-release ethic. It has implications for how harvest regulations are
received and for messaging around selective harvest, but the change is gradual
rather than abrupt.

## Bottom line

Over the past two decades, Nebraska's licensed anglers have become **more active,
consistently satisfied, less reliant on motorized boats, broader in the species
they pursue, and somewhat less focused on harvest**. The throughline for
management is that anglers increasingly value **access and a quality overall
experience across a diversity of waters**, alongside the traditional draw of
premier species. Continued attention to shore and small-craft access, dependable
fishing close to home, and the broader setting will serve the largest and
fastest-growing segments of the angling public.
